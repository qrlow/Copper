    How Scenarios Are Built

    Assumptions, not sourced forecasts

    The same public data baseline is used in every scenario: ICSG 2024 refined usage, USGS MCS 2026 2024 world mine/refinery supply, and World Bank macro drivers.

    The World Bank macro growth rates are fixed historical lookback rates applied across the forecast horizon; they are not rolling future forecasts.

    The demand-driver weights are scenario assumptions. A regression diagnostic is shown below, but it is not used as the forecast weighting because fit is weak and the macro predictors overlap mechanically.

    The scenarios change only the assumption knobs: demand growth, transition-demand bonus, mine/refinery growth, disruption loss, and scrap/recycling response.

    Bull means stronger demand and tighter supply. Bear means weaker demand and looser supply. Base sits between them.

    How The Dataset Is Built

    Reproducible CSV
    1. Transcribe annual world mine production, refined production, and refined usage from the ICSG Factbook annex for 1960-2024.
    2. Fetch World Bank world real GDP, industry value-added share of GDP, and population.
    3. Calculate industry activity as real GDP multiplied by industry share of GDP.
    4. Calculate GDP per capita as real GDP divided by population.
    5. Convert usage and macro variables into growth rates where each test has overlapping data. The world-GDP sample runs from 1961-2024. The multivariate industry/GDP-per-capita sample starts in 1992 because World Bank world industry-share data begins in 1991.

    Statistical Considerations

    Why not use it
    • Better GDP test: using total world real GDP growth gives annual R-squared of --, materially better than GDP per capita alone.
    • Rule-of-thumb result: when the intercept is forced to zero, a 1 percentage point move in world real GDP growth maps to about 0.93 percentage points of world refined copper usage growth.
    • Cycle smoothing helps: using 5-year CAGRs raises the fit, which suggests the GDP link is clearer over economic cycles than in single-year changes.
    • Predictor overlap: GDP = GDP per capita * population, so population is mechanically related to GDP per capita.
    • Industry activity overlap: industry activity = GDP * industry share, so the industry driver already contains the GDP component.
    • Global time-series sample: the regression has a small annual sample and no country-level panel variation.
    • Missing copper-specific drivers: prices, substitution, sector mix, grid investment, China property cycles, policy, and inventory changes are not included.
